Architecture

The ReMeta toolbox allows researchers to estimate latent parameters of observers that perform a decision-making task involving confidence ratings. Type 1 parameters refer to parameters that describe the decision-making process, while Type 2 parameters refer to parameters that describe the confidence rating process. The focus of Remeta is an estimate of metacognitive noise, i.e. how accurately observers can monitor their own performance, and metacognitive bias, i.e. whether observers tend to be over- or under-confident.

The basic architecture of the toolbox is shown in the figure below.

Based on a stimulus input xxx, the observer computes a decision value yyy, the sign of which determines a type 1 decision DDD. This process can be characterized by a variety of type 1 parameters, including type 1 noise σ1\sigma_{1}σ1​ (sensory or decisional noise) and a type 1 decision bias δ1\delta_1δ1​.

The absolute value of the noisy decision value — labeled type 1 evidence z1z_1z1​ — is the relevant input to the type 2 level. This “readout” may not be perfect though and could be subject to some readout noise σ2\sigma_2σ2​ and a multiplicative bias φ2\varphi_2φ2​. Thus, the evidence at the type 2 stage — type 2 evidence z2z_2z2​ — may be different from z1z_1z1​. A model with unreliable readout is referred to as noisy-readout model.

By design, the model assumes that observers represent confidence as probability correct, thus computing c=p(correct∣z2,σ^1)c=p(\text{correct}\mid z_2,\hat{\sigma}_1)c=p(correct∣z2​,σ^1​). This is sometimes referred to as Bayesian confidence. Nevertheless, for this computation, the observer needs an estimate σ^1\hat{\sigma}_1σ^1​ of their own type 1 noise σ1\sigma_1σ1​.

Estimating one’s own type 1 noise σ1\sigma_1σ1​ is far from trivial and may thus plausibly be subject to some noise itself. This is a second form of metacognitive noise that was suggested by Boundy-Singer et al. (2022). Here, this is referred to as noisy-temperature model.

Finally, internal representations of confidence need to be communicated to the experiementer. This likewise is a lossy process, for two reasons: 1) the most confidence scales are discrete and hence required confidence criteria γ1,…,γn\gamma_1, \dots, \gamma_nγ1​,…,γn​ to map a continuous internal estimate to a finite number of response options; 2) the mapping itself may be noisy. This last form of metacognitive noise is referred to as a noisy-report model.

In ReMeta, the researcher must decide for which of these three sources of metacognitive noise they consider most dominant or most interesting, since only one can be modeled at a time.
